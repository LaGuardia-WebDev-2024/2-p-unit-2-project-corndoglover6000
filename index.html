Fantastically Fractal Ferns

Finding Ferns

Ferns are a common sight in many habitats: woodlands, mountains, deserts, open fields, and even concrete. You can find these vascular plants with seeds on their undersides, or in the process of unfurling. But some people don't know about the awesome geometry of ferns.

Natural Fractals

Fractals are defined as “a rough or fragmented geometric shape that can be subdivided in parts, each of which is a reduced-size copy of the whole.” by Benoît Mandelbrot, the mathmetician who coined the term fractal. To simplify this definition, a fractal is a self-similar shape that resembles itself wether you zoom in or out.
Some common examples of naturally occuring fractals are:

  • snowflakes
  • the branching of a tree
  • rivers splitting into smaller rivers
  • lightning
  • blood vessels
  • seedheads
  • crystals
  • and of course, ferns

Barnsley Fractal

Ferns are perfect examples of naturally occuring fractals. The spiral-shaped fiddlehead of a sprouting fern is formed by the leaf stalk as it unfurls into a frond. As the leaf stalk grows, each leaflet first appears as its own mini fiddlehead. Ferns are such good fractals that people have mathematically generated them as such.
